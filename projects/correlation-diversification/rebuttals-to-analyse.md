# Rebuttal Arguments to Address

Distilled from Wine Bourse (LinkedIn post & blog) and Sara Danese (LinkedIn post & "In The Mood For Wine" blog). Duplicates and overlapping points have been merged into distinct arguments.

---

## Argument 1: Liv-ex index outperformance is a GBP currency illusion

**Source:** Wine Bourse (LI post & blog), Sara Danese (LI post & blog)

**The claim:** Fine wine indices are quoted in GBP, but the underlying wines are overwhelmingly EUR-denominated assets. During periods of financial stress (2008 crisis, Brexit, COVID), GBP weakened significantly against EUR. The apparent outperformance of fine wine indices during these periods is simply a reflection of an embedded short-GBP/long-EUR currency position, not genuine asset resilience.

**Supporting data cited:**
- Liv-ex 1000 up 22% in GBP in 2016 vs only 5% in EUR — the 17pp gap was sterling depreciation
- Over 20 years the main Liv-ex index returned 3.6x in GBP, but only 3x in EUR and 2.6x in USD
- GBP return ≈ EUR return + change in EUR/GBP

---

## Argument 2: Fine wine is too illiquid and risky to be held during crises

**Source:** Wine Bourse (LI post & blog), Sara Danese (blog)

**The claim:** Fine wine is highly illiquid with wide bid-offer spreads, non-income-generating, and traded in a largely unregulated environment. These are the exact opposite characteristics of what investors want during market stress. Fine wine is "the polar opposite of where people should be investing during times of crisis."

**Supporting data cited:**
- When UK buyers lost ~20% purchasing power around Brexit, trading volumes would be expected to fall
- Wide bid-offer spreads in an unregulated market

---

## Argument 3: Diversification claims constitute mis-selling

**Source:** Wine Bourse (LI post & blog)

**The claim:** Presenting fine wine as a diversification play using GBP-denominated index charts during crisis periods is misleading — and because fine wine is unregulated, these practices go unchecked. This is described as "a blatant example of mis-selling" that would not be permitted in regulated markets.

**Supporting data cited:**
- Oeno's failure — investors reportedly lost 80%+ of capital
- Fine wine investment is not regulated

---

## Argument 4: Currency exposure is a risk factor, not a feature

**Source:** Sara Danese (LI post & blog)

**The claim:** Investors holding wine are implicitly taking a currency position (long EUR, short their home currency). This is a real risk that should be understood and disclosed, not presented as a diversification benefit. The same currency dynamics that flatter GBP returns when sterling weakens will hurt returns when sterling strengthens.

**Supporting data cited:**
- Analogy to S&P 500: a GBP investor buying US equities is also taking a USD/GBP bet — net return was ~8pp lower when the dollar fell against sterling
- Wine production inputs (labour, glass, cork, shipping) all priced in EUR
- ~45% of Liv-ex trading is European, ~40% is UK-based

---

## Argument 5: Fine wine is exposed to geopolitical and trade policy risk

**Source:** Sara Danese (blog)

**The claim:** Wine is vulnerable to tariffs and trade wars precisely because it is economically marginal but politically convenient as a pressure tool. This geopolitical exposure undermines arguments that wine is a store of value or hedge against political shocks.

**Supporting data cited:**
- China's ban on Australian wine
- Trump 200% tariff threat on Champagne, subsequent 15% tariffs
- USD weakened ~10% since tariff announcements, creating a "double whammy" for US buyers (tariff + currency)
- VOS Selections Supreme Court case challenging IEEPA tariff authority
- France: wine & spirits are the country's 3rd largest export category

---

## Argument 6: Caution against presenting wine as a safe haven based on index history

**Source:** Sara Danese (blog)

**The claim:** Because of their construction (EUR asset quoted in GBP), Liv-ex indices can look like they're rallying during periods of sterling weakness. Taking this at face value and calling it "wine going up" is misleading. Wine should not be presented as a scarce store of value or hedge against political shocks based solely on historical Liv-ex index performance.

**Key quote:** "Anyone who imports or exports wine knows this instinctively. In periods of political instability and currency stress, wine does not behave like a safe haven."

## Summary: Core themes to rebut

| # | Theme | Difficulty to rebut |
|---|-------|-------------------|
| 1 | GBP currency illusion inflates apparent returns | Medium — valid point, but doesn't eliminate diversification entirely |
| 2 | Illiquidity makes wine unsuitable in crises | Medium — illiquidity ≠ price collapse; needs data |
| 3 | Mis-selling / regulatory gap | Low relevance — about bad actors, not about the asset class itself |
| 4 | Currency exposure is a risk, not a benefit | Medium — true for any international asset; can be reframed |
| 5 | Geopolitical / tariff vulnerability | Low-Medium — affects the wine *trade*, not necessarily secondary market prices |
| 6 | Liv-ex indices are misleading benchmarks for wine investment | High opportunity — this actually supports WineFi's heterogeneity argument |

**Note:** Arguments 1, 4, and 6 are closely related (all centre on currency/index construction). They could be addressed together. Argument 3 is about industry ethics rather than asset fundamentals — probably acknowledge and move past. Argument 6 actually plays into WineFi's core thesis: that index-level analysis undersells what disciplined selection can achieve.
