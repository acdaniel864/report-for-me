# TLDR

- Whilst fine wine is not isolated from financial markets**, it has historically shown low and time-varying correlation with equities and gold**. Correlation is not persistently high and meaningfully differs by period, region, and index construction.
- **During major market stress**, fine wine has often shown **apparent resilience.**
    - During the Great Financial Crisis (2008–09) the Liv-ex 100 fell ~17% vs S&P 500 ~36%, recovering to prior highs by 2010 (equities not until 2013).
- Static month-on-month correlations are misleading. Illiquidity, appraisal-based pricing, and non-synchronous trading artificially smooth wine prices and bias correlations downward. **Rolling correlations (1–3 years) provide a more realistic view, showing low but unstable relationships that can briefly rise during systemic crises** (e.g. correlation >0.5 during 2008).
- Fine wine is highly heterogeneous. Correlation with mainstream assets, as well as overall returns, varies wildly by region, producer, liquidity and other factors.
- Diversification benefits are selection-dependent. Broader indices (Liv-ex 1000) and regional exposure (e.g. Burgundy 150) have historically shown lower equity correlation than the Liv-ex 100. **A well-selected portfolio can provide diversification and resilience.**

# Introduction

Establishing the long-term relationship between fine wine and mainstream asset classes is complicated by wine’s relatively short history as a financial asset. With ~25 years of market tracking index data, the ability to draw definitive conclusions is limited, and, as ever, past performance provides no guarantee of future outcomes.

Measurement choices further complicate the analysis. Which wines are included, and how the fine wine market is defined, materially influence observed correlations. For example, Bordeaux prices tend to exhibit higher correlation with mainstream asset classes than Burgundian wines.

This report first, examines on a causal basis the arguments for and against fine wine prices correlating with mainstream asset classes. 

Second, it evaluates what the available data suggests about the degree of correlation that does exist. 

Third, it considers whether fine wine functions as a safe-haven asset and whether it plays a legitimate role as a portfolio diversifier. 

Forth, it outlines the important role ***wine selection*** has in building a fine wine portfolio that is robust to wider financial market downturns. 

Finally, it outlines additional interesting findings and areas that warrant further investigation.

# The Theory

**On a causation basis, several factors that affect other asset classes also affect wine:**

1. **Money supply:** More money circulating in the economy often leads to increased investment capital flowing into assets like fine wine, pushing up prices, as seen during the COVID pandemic. A reduced money supply dampens this demand.
2. **Interest rates:** Higher rates increase the *opportunity cost* of holding non-yielding assets like wine (as cash and bonds offer higher returns) and make borrowing to invest more expensive. Lower rates have the inverse effect, making wine relatively more attractive.
3. **The wealth effect:** When people *feel* wealthier due to rising stock portfolios or property values, they spend and invest more confidently in luxury items like fine wine. Conversely, market downturns make people feel poorer, reducing luxury spending and impacting demand from both individuals and the hospitality sector (restaurants and hotels).
4. **Currency movements:** particularly shifting global purchasing power into the UK trading hub. We see when GBP weakens in real terms, UK-denominated fine wine becomes more attractive to international buyers, supporting prices and returns.
5. **Credit stress:** When financing conditions tighten and risk premia rise, fine wine markets come under pressure as investor risk appetite declines and market liquidity deteriorates.

**Supply-side factors unique to wine that can lead to price movements uncorrelated with other asset classes:**

1. **Crop yields:** The quantity of crop yield, driven by region-specific weather, significantly impacts scarcity. The volume of each vintage is dictated by weather conditions; lower (or higher) crop yields—especially in succession—reduce (or increase) supply, leading to upward (or downward) price pressure.
2. **Climate change impacts:** “The last great vintage.” Longer-term climate shifts affect production consistency and quality, potentially making top-tier vintages rarer. As producers adapt production techniques, the wines themselves change. Climate change can also prompt revisions to local regulations (e.g., potential changes to permitted grape varieties in Burgundy) in response to viticultural challenges. Incidentally, Château Lafleur [announced it is leaving the Pomerol and Bordeaux appellation systems](https://www.decanter.com/wine-news/chateau-lafleur-to-withdraw-from-bordeaux-and-pomerol-appellations-563548/), citing climate-related motivations. The estate wants the freedom to adapt to heat and drought without the constraints of AOC regulations.
3. **Merchant inventory management:** Merchants’ buying decisions significantly influence short-term availability and prices. When merchants collectively over-buy or under-buy, certain wines or at large, transactional prices are affected in the short term—even if total market supply remains unchanged—as they manage stock levels and balance their books. This has been a massive driving force in both the boom and bust seen during, and in the wake of, COVID. 

**On the demand side, significant uncorrelated drivers include:**

1. **Vintage variation:** The *quality* of crop yield, also driven by region-specific weather, significantly impacts desirability. For example, “good years” justify higher prices and prompt higher demand, but consecutive “good years” can dilute the uniqueness of each. Conversely, a “legendary” vintage—especially a low-yielding one (e.g., Champagne 2008)—surrounded by lesser vintages is both scarce and highly sought after.
2. **Idiosyncratic consumption trends:** Specific, often culturally driven, shifts in taste can create demand booms—for example, the surge in Chinese interest in high-end Bordeaux from 2009–2011.
3. **Changing producer reputation:** The reputation of a producer or brand evolves over time based on factors such as consistency of quality (or lack thereof), changes in ownership or management, investment in facilities, the arrival or departure of a star winemaker, or awards and external recognition.
4. **Emerging market adoption:** As developing economies (e.g., Brazil, India) grow in wealth, fine wine consumption often increases as it gains cultural value, creating new demand sources that may be out of sync with established market cycles.
5. **Veblen good dynamics:** Many prestigious fine wines function as Veblen goods. Their high prices signal exclusivity and status, which can bolster brand prestige and *increase* demand among consumers, regardless of broader economic sentiment.

### **Static Correlation on a Month-by-Month Basis**

This correlation matrix heat-map compares various asset classes, including mainstream financial assets in terms of their correlation of returns month to month. 

Fine wine is represented by the Liv-ex 100 index. The Liv-ex 100 index is designed to be responsive to current market conditions; it contains 100 of the most in-demand, highly transacted fine wines and trading volume is used to weight the components. Component valuations are based on appraisals via [Liv-ex mid-price](https://www.liv-ex.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/10/midprice.pdf) defined as “The mid-point between the highest live bid and lowest live offer on the market”. Prices are based not just on offers (sticky to down-market conditions) but also bids which as a representation of demand are thought to be highly responsive to market conditions.

In its short history (since 2002), it is arguable that the Liv-ex 100 has demonstrated this responsiveness. During the 2008 crisis from September to October, the Liv-ex 100 fell 16%, closely mirroring the S&P 500's 16% drop over the same period. No obvious lag was present here at a monthly granularity. Furthermore, during the subsequent recovery (Jan 2009 - Jul 2011), the index climbed 73%, moving largely in tandem with gold (+69.5%)—performance not characteristic of an obviously lagging indicator.

There are issues with this analysis however:

1. **Auto-correlation of prices:** Wine valuations are heavily anchored to prior transactions. In sequential sales, previous prices are often used as benchmarks (auto-correlation). Meaning there is reason to expect any appraisal of fine wine prices to lag behind other asset classes.
2. **Illiquidity and non-continuous price discovery:** Wine is illiquid, it does not benefit from continuous trading like other assets and some valuations in Liv-ex indices are based on bid-offer spreads not realised trades. 

These factors result in artificially smooth price data and lagging price changes, both of which significantly understate true volatility and bias correlation downward.

**Why is this a problem?** 

- Correlation between wine and liquid assets may be **artificially low**
- Volatility of wine returns is likely **understated**
- Diversification benefits can be **overstated**
- Risk-return characteristics may appear **more favourable than they are in reality**

Additionally, analysing static snapshots—like the plot above—can give a misleading impression of wine’s diversification potential. What really matters is how wine correlates with other assets over the full investment period.

### **Rolling Correlation Fine Wine vs Equities and Gold**

This section analyses rolling correlation of fine wine with equites and gold with a view to: 

1. **Avoid misleading static snapshots:** Rolling correlations capture how the relationship between assets changes, highlighting when diversification persist and when it doesn’t. Capturing changing market dynamics rather than averaging them into a single number.
2. **Reduce the smoothing effect of artificially low correlations:** Assuming the Liv-ex 100 tends to adjust slowly and can be influenced by infrequent or anomalous trades, rolling correlation helps to capture these gradual movements over 1-3 year periods. 
3. **Provide a more robust test of diversification:** Rather than assuming wine is a consistent diversifier, rolling correlation allows us to examine whether it behaves that way precisely when traditional assets are declining.

Low correlation alone isn’t enough though. 

An asset needs to also offer a sufficient expected return. Reducing portfolio volatility only matters if it improves overall risk-adjusted performance. Adding low-return assets may smooth returns, but risks weakening long-term growth.

- Uncorrelated returns are only useful if, when one asset falls, the other holds steady or rises in value.
- Negative correlation is even more valuable, but only if it persists during drawdowns — one asset appreciates while the other asset is falling.

The Liv-ex 100 fell by just 1%. While this stock market correction may have been too short-lived for a full adjustment in wine indices, the Liv-ex mid-price—based on the most actively traded wines and weighted by liquidity—should, in theory, respond to a wealth effect. 

A sharp equity sell-off would be expected to soften bids, suggesting we might anticipate some short-term reaction in wine prices during such corrections.

**December 2021 to Sept 2022 — S&P 500 falls 24%:**

While wine was witnessing a bull-run from 2020 to 2023 at the end of December both equities and gold fell in unison. With S&P 500 falling 24% and gold 9%, the Liv-ex 100 rose 9%. 

Trades were realised at the peak of the wine market, including many involving Liv-ex index components.

This was followed by a wine market crash and a stock market rally.

### Heterogeneity Within Fine Wine

Fine wine is not homogenous asset, the importance of asset selection within fine wine cannot be overstated. Quarter-on-quarter we tend to see ~20-30% of investment-grade wines increasing in price while ~20-30% fall in price and the remainder stay the same. Within Domaine de la Romanée-Conti (DRC) *alone*, from 2021 to 2026 the mean returns spread between the top and bottom half of wines was 52%. Which wines you choose to invest in and the prices you buy them at is all important. This kind of heterogeneity extends to identifying wines with low correlation to the wider financial markets. 

**Broader Fine Wine Index**

The Liv-ex 1000, a *broader* measure of the fine wine markets incorporating 900 more wines than the Liv-ex 100, shows even greater resilience to stock market downturns and higher long term growth.

### **Rolling Correlation - Fine Wine and Gold**

Gold is considered one of the quintessential safe haven assets—frequently sought during periods of market turbulence.

Since the inception of the Liv-ex 1000 index until the covid pandemic, fine wine did not act dissimilarly to gold. 

Both fell but recovered more quickly than equities in the wake of the 2008 financial crisis and saw consistent growth for the subsequent 4 years.

Following that the wine market plateaued from August 2011 until August 2016, while gold fell 19.7% over the same period. 

In the case of wine this was largely down to a stagnant Bordeaux market and the Liv-ex indices being heavily weighted towards Bordelaise wines.

Again, the specific wines used to represent the fine wine market play a significant role. From 2016 to until covid both gold and fine wine rallied. While gold crashed briefly at the height of COVID, fine wine peaked, which, of course, has been followed by a rally in gold and a slow correction of fine wine while a glut of supply has worked through the system.

In the modern era, there is no such thing as an isolated asset class. Financial markets are highly inter-related, driven by a massive underlying financial and monetary forces.

The evidence in this report suggests that fine wine has historically exhibited **low and time-varying correlation** with mainstream financial assets, and has shown **apparent resilience during several periods of market stress**, with shallower drawdowns and, in some cases, faster recoveries than equities. This behaviour reflects wine’s dual nature: influenced by macro-financial conditions, yet also shaped by distinct factors that are largely independent of traditional capital markets.

Fine wine is not a homogenous asset class, nor is low correlation guaranteed. Month-on-month static correlations parroted by legacy wine investment firms can statistically overstate fine wine’s diversification potential. Rolling correlations offer a more dynamic and realistic lens, revealing that wine exhibits low and inconsistent correlation with equities and gold over multi-year horizons.

Crucially, the diversification benefits of fine wine are **selection-dependent**. Returns, volatility, and correlation vary significantly across regions and even producers. Portfolio construction matters. A carefully selected fine wine portfolio can provide meaningful diversification and resilience, while a poorly selected one may behave much like a cyclical luxury asset.

Fine wine is not as a guaranteed safe haven but through disciplined data-led selection and purchasing - rather than passive exposure to the market as a whole - low correlation diversification benefits can be achieved.
